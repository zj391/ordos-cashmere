# How to Verify Ordos Cashmere: 7 Tests Every B2B Buyer Should Run

Origin fraud in the cashmere trade is real. A 2023 industry survey estimated 25-35% of "Mongolian cashmere" sold in Western markets is either blended with non-Mongolian fiber, chemically treated, or both. For B2B buyers paying $100+ per kg for premium fiber, that adds up fast.

This guide covers the 7 tests that separate real Ordos cashmere from clever marketing — most cost under $200 and can be done before your first shipment.

## Test 1: OFDA micron scan (the gold standard)

The single most important cashmere test. **OFDA 2000** is a laser-scanning instrument that measures fiber diameter on 2,000+ fibers per sample, producing a mean and standard deviation.

**What to look for**:
- **Premium white cashmere**: 14.5-15.5μm mean, σ < 1.0μm
- **Standard white**: 15.0-16.0μm mean, σ < 1.2μm
- **Anything > 16.5μm** marketed as "premium" is suspect

**Cost**: $50-150 per sample. Most universities with textile engineering programs offer OFDA testing for under $100.

**Red flag**: A supplier who provides only "typical values" rather than your-batch-specific OFDA data.

## Test 2: Hand-combing visual inspection

Before any lab work, look at the raw combings. Real Albus goat fiber is:
- **Long** (36-42mm for premium, 32-38mm for standard)
- **Parallel-aligned** (herders comb in one direction)
- **Slightly bluish-tinted** (true white Mongolian has a natural cast, not pure bright white)
- **Has a soft hand feel** (low micron + good crimp)

Pure bright white fiber has usually been bleached, which damages the cuticle. If you run your fingers through a sample and get a "squeaky" or "plastic" feel, it's been chemically treated.

## Test 3: Dehaired yield check

Take 1 kg of greasy cashmere and send it to a processing facility for dehairing. The yield (dehaired output weight / raw input weight) reveals a lot:

- **35-42%** yield: high-quality Albus fiber, properly combed
- **25-35%** yield: standard Mongolian, possibly mixed grades
- **< 25%** yield: very short fiber or excessive guard hair (low quality)

If a supplier claims 50% yield, they're either chemically treating the fiber (which damages it) or blending with non-cashmere.

## Test 4: Color consistency within bale

A real "white" cashmere bale should have **subtle but visible color variation** within it — different goats, different body parts, slightly different ages all contribute. Perfectly uniform bright-white fiber is a sign of bleaching.

Take 5-10 random pinches from different parts of a bale. If they look like they came from identical sources, the bale has likely been processed (blended, sorted, or bleached) to remove natural variation.

## Test 5: Length distribution (Hauteur)

Use a **comb sorter** or send to a lab for **Almeter** testing. Length distribution reveals:

- **Hauteur (H) > 36mm**: premium, suitable for worsted spinning
- **H 32-36mm**: standard, suitable for woolen spinning
- **H < 32mm**: short fiber, only suitable for felted products or low-end blends

Short fiber shows up in finished yarn as pilling and weak spots — exactly what premium brands are trying to avoid.

## Test 6: Guard hair content (visual microscope)

Under 40x magnification, count guard hair fibers vs cashmere fibers in a 1g sample. Real dehaired cashmere should have **< 2% guard hair by count**. Anything above 5% suggests incomplete dehairing.

You don't need an expensive microscope — a $30 USB microscope with 40-100x magnification works fine. Count 100 fibers, classify each as cashmere (fine, crimped) or guard hair (thick, straight).

## Test 7: Supplier facility audit

The ultimate test: **visit the factory**. In one day at a vertically integrated cashmere facility, you can verify:

- **Herder contracts** on file (real direct sourcing)
- **Dehairing line** in operation (not just a trading company)
- **Bale tracking system** (every bale tagged from herder to customer)
- **OFDA equipment** in-house (means they test, not just claim)
- **Storage conditions** (climate-controlled, no direct sunlight)

A reputable supplier will arrange a 2-day facility visit including Ordos and a herder village. If a supplier makes excuses about visiting, walk away.

## Red flags that bypass any test

Even the best tests can be fooled if the supplier is determined. Watch for these behavioral red flags:

- **Refuses to provide per-bale OFDA data** ("We test but don't share results")
- **Asks for 100% upfront payment** before any samples
- **Refuses factory visit** or limits it to "office meetings" in Beijing
- **Sample quality doesn't match bulk order quality** (the classic "golden sample" scam)
- **Pricing significantly below market** ($60/kg for "premium white" is too good to be true)

## What to do if you suspect fraud

1. **Document everything**: emails, photos, video, OFDA data
2. **Test every shipment** — don't trust previous quality holds
3. **Build relationships with multiple suppliers** so you can switch quickly
4. **Use escrow or L/C at sight** for new suppliers under $50K
5. **Report to industry bodies** — The Cashmere and Camel Hair Manufacturers Institute (CCMI) maintains a supplier database

## The cost of verification vs. the cost of fraud

A 7-test verification protocol costs $300-500 per supplier. A single bad shipment of "premium white" that turns out to be 50/50 white/brown blend costs $5,000-20,000 in lost margin, plus customer trust.

**Verification is always cheaper than fraud.**
