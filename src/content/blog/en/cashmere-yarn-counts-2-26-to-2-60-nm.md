# Cashmere Yarn Count Explained: 2/26 to 2/60 Nm

"100% cashmere yarn" tells you the fiber. The **count** tells you how fine or thick the yarn is, which determines the gauge, hand feel, and end-use of your finished product.

A wrong count choice means a sweater that's too heavy for the design, or a scarf that's too flimsy. This guide covers the practical implications of cashmere yarn counts from 2/26 Nm to 2/60 Nm.

## What does "2/48 Nm" actually mean?

Cashmere yarn counts use the **Nm** (Number metric) system. The format is **plies/fineness**:
- **2/** means 2-ply (two single yarns twisted together)
- **48** means 48,000 meters of yarn weighs 1 kg

So a **2/48 Nm cashmere yarn** is a 2-ply yarn where 48 km of the yarn weighs 1 kg. **Higher Nm = finer yarn**.

The count you choose depends on the **gauge** (knits per inch) of your final product:

| Count (Nm) | Weight per meter | Gauge | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2/26 | ~0.077 g/m | 5-7 | Chunky sweaters, blankets, heavy scarves |
| 2/32 | ~0.063 g/m | 7-9 | Mid-gauge sweaters, medium scarves |
| 2/36 | ~0.056 g/m | 9-12 | Standard sweaters |
| 2/48 | ~0.042 g/m | 12-14 | Fine-gauge sweaters, light scarves |
| 2/60 | ~0.033 g/m | 14-18 | Ultra-fine knits, premium scarves |
| 2/80 (custom) | ~0.025 g/m | 18+ | Sheer fabrics, exceptional luxury |

## Match yarn count to gauge

The relationship between yarn count and knitting gauge is roughly inverse. Higher Nm = finer yarn = more stitches per inch for a given fabric weight.

**Practical rule of thumb**:
- For 5-gauge chunky: use 2/26 to 2/30 Nm
- For 7-gauge mid: use 2/30 to 2/36 Nm
- For 9-gauge standard: use 2/36 to 2/40 Nm
- For 12-gauge fine: use 2/40 to 2/48 Nm
- For 14-gauge extra fine: use 2/48 to 2/60 Nm
- For 18-gauge sheer: use 2/60 to 2/80 Nm

Deviating too far in either direction gives you either a stiff, boardy fabric (count too low) or a flimsy, see-through fabric (count too high).

## What 2/26 vs 2/60 means in hand feel

A 2/26 Nm cashmere yarn produces a **chunky, robust fabric** with substantial hand feel. The yarn is thick, the fabric has body, and the finished sweater has a "cozy" look. Think heavy winter cardigans or boatneck sweaters.

A 2/60 Nm cashmere yarn produces a **fine, drapey fabric** with a silky hand feel. The yarn is barely visible to the eye, the fabric flows, and the finished garment has a "luxury" look. Think thin V-necks, fine-gauge scarves, or layering pieces.

**Most luxury B2B orders** are 2/48 to 2/60 Nm. These counts give the smooth, drapey, "soft cashmere" feel that justifies the premium price.

**Mid-market and casual** tends toward 2/26 to 2/36 Nm. These are still soft, but with more body and a relaxed look.

## Worsted vs woolen: count implications

The count you choose interacts with the spinning method:

- **Worsted spinning** requires longer fiber (Hauteur > 36mm) and is more common in finer counts (2/40 to 2/60+). Worsted yarn is smoother and stronger.
- **Woolen spinning** tolerates shorter fiber and is more common in heavier counts (2/26 to 2/40). Woolen yarn is softer and loftier.

A 2/60 Nm **woolen-spun** yarn is rare and requires exceptional fiber quality. Most 2/60 Nm is worsted-spun. Conversely, a 2/26 Nm **worsted-spun** yarn is possible but unusual — most 2/26 is woolen.

## Custom counts (2/16 to 2/80)

Standard counts (2/26 to 2/60 Nm) cover ~90% of B2B demand. For specialty products, custom counts are possible:

- **2/16 to 2/24**: Very heavy, used for blankets, heavy outerwear, and upholstery. Custom orders usually 200kg+.
- **2/80 to 2/120**: Ultra-fine, used for sheer woven fabrics and exceptional luxury pieces. Lead time +30 days; custom orders 100kg+.

For custom counts, add +20-30% lead time and expect 200kg+ MOQ.

## How to test yarn count before committing

A simple test for any sample cone:
1. **Measure 10 meters** of yarn
2. **Weigh it** (use a milligram scale for accuracy)
3. **Calculate**: count = 10,000 / weight_in_grams × 2 (since 2-ply)
4. **Compare to claimed count**: should be within ±2%

A supplier claiming 2/48 Nm but testing at 2/32 Nm is a quality red flag. Always test samples before placing bulk orders.

## Mixing Ordos and Mongolian cashmere for unique yarn

Some premium B2B buyers request **mixed Ordos Mongolian cashmere yarn** — combining fibers from both Inner Mongolia and Mongolia proper. The goal is a unique hand feel that combines:

- **Ordos fiber**: Slightly higher crimp, more "spring" in the yarn
- **Mongolian fiber**: Slightly longer staple, more "drape" in the fabric

The result is a yarn with both properties — soft and lofty *and* drapey. It's a subtle difference (most customers won't notice) but distinctive enough to justify a "signature yarn" marketing claim.

Mixed Ordos Mongolian yarn requires **dual sourcing** from both regions and careful blending at the dehairing stage. Most Ordos factories can do this, but you need to specify it explicitly.
