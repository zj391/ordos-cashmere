# OEKO-TEX Certified Cashmere: What B2B Buyers Need to Know

Cashmere is a natural fiber. But the journey from goat to garment involves **chemicals** — scouring agents, dyes, finishing treatments, anti-pilling enzymes. Some of these chemicals can leave residues that are harmful to human health.

For B2B buyers exporting to the EU, US, or Japan, textile safety certifications are increasingly **legally required or buyer-mandated**. This guide covers what certifications actually mean, which ones matter, and how to verify claims.

## The 7 main textile safety certifications

### 1. **OEKO-TEX Standard 100** (most important)

**What it tests**: Over 300 substances including formaldehyde, heavy metals (lead, cadmium, mercury), pesticides, chlorinated phenols, phthalates, and azo dyes.

**Levels**:
- **Class I** (baby): strictest, < 0.5% threshold for most substances
- **Class II** (skin contact): standard for most adult apparel
- **Class III** (no direct skin contact): home textiles
- **Class IV** (decoration only): rarely used

**What it doesn't test**: Animal welfare, environmental impact, fiber origin.

**Cost to factory**: $2,000-5,000 per year + testing fees per shipment.

### 2. **GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard)**

**What it certifies**: Organic fiber content (≥70% for 'organic', ≥95% for 'organic' label) + environmental processing + social criteria.

**What it doesn't certify**: Animal welfare (cashmere goats are not covered by GOTS even when fiber is "organic" — this is a known gap).

**Cost to factory**: $3,000-8,000 per year + chain-of-custody documentation.

### 3. **RWS (Responsible Wool Standard)**

**What it certifies**: Animal welfare + land management + chain of custody for wool (and recently, cashmere).

**What it doesn't certify**: Chemical safety (no substance testing like OEKO-TEX).

**Cost to factory**: $2,500-6,000 per year.

### 4. **ISO 9001** (Quality Management)

**What it certifies**: Quality management systems, not product safety.

**What it doesn't certify**: Anything about the actual cashmere or chemicals used.

**Cost to factory**: $3,000-15,000 initial + annual audits.

### 5. **ISO 14001** (Environmental Management)

**What it certifies**: Environmental management systems.

**What it doesn't certify**: Product safety or fiber origin.

### 6. **BSCI / Sedex SMETA** (Social Compliance)

**What they certify**: Worker welfare, fair wages, no child labor.

**What they don't certify**: Product safety or fiber origin.

### 7. **GCS (Good Cashmere Standard)** (industry-specific)

**What it certifies**: Cashmere fiber content + animal welfare + traceability from farm to finished product.

**What it doesn't certify**: Chemical safety (no substance testing).

## What certifications actually matter for cashmere

For B2B buyers in different markets:

| Market | Required/Mandated | Recommended |
|---|---|---|
| **EU (all markets)** | REACH compliance, OEKO-TEX (most retailers mandate) | GOTS, RWS |
| **US** | CPSIA compliance, OEKO-TEX (department stores mandate) | RWS, GOTS |
| **Japan** | OEKO-TEX (standard), JIS L 0217 (Japanese standard) | RWS |
| **China (domestic)** | GB 18401 (China textile safety standard) | OEKO-TEX |
| **Korea** | KC Mark (similar to OEKO-TEX) | RWS |

**For 90% of B2B buyers, OEKO-TEX Standard 100 (Class II) is the minimum bar.** Anything below that and you can't sell to most EU/US department stores and luxury retailers.

## How to verify certification claims

Any factory can claim certifications. Here's how to verify:

### Step 1: Ask for the certificate number

Every OEKO-TEX certificate has a unique number. Ask the supplier for the certificate number and the certifying body (e.g., "Hohenstein Institute" or "Testex").

### Step 2: Look up on the certifying body's website

- **OEKO-TEX**: https://www.oeko-tex.com/en/buyers/search (search by company name or certificate number)
- **GOTS**: https://www.global-standard.org/find-suppliers.html
- **RWS**: https://textileexchange.org/certificate-search/
- **BSCI**: https://www.bsciplatform.com/

If the certificate doesn't show up, the claim is bogus.

### Step 3: Verify the certificate scope

Certificates cover specific product categories. A factory's OEKO-TEX certificate might cover "yarn and fabric" but not "garments" — meaning their finished knitwear isn't actually certified.

### Step 4: Check expiration dates

OEKO-TEX certificates are typically valid for 1 year. A certificate that "expired 6 months ago" is technically invalid.

## What to do if your supplier fakes certifications

It's more common than you'd think. In 2023, an industry survey estimated 15-20% of "OEKO-TEX certified" claims from Chinese suppliers were not verifiable.

If you suspect fraud:
1. **Request the certificate PDF** (not just a number) and check the URL it points to
2. **Verify the certificate number** on the certifying body's website
3. **Commission your own test** on a sample shipment ($200-500 per test)
4. **Report to the certifying body** if fraud is confirmed — they can revoke the supplier's status

## How to read a typical OEKO-TEX certificate

A standard certificate includes:
- **Certificate number** (e.g., "SH015 123456")
- **Issue date** and **validity** (usually 1 year)
- **Holder** (the certified company)
- **Product class** (I, II, III, or IV)
- **Tested articles** (yarn, fabric, garment, etc.)
- **Testing institute** (Hohenstein, Testex, etc.)
- **Authorized signature** and stamp

If any of these are missing or look altered, request a fresh copy directly from the certifying body.
