## Why Ordos Cashmere Matters

Ordos, located in Inner Mongolia, is the world's largest cashmere production hub. The region's unique climate—cold, dry, and high-altitude—produces cashmere goats with exceptionally fine undercoat fibers. Ordos cashmere typically measures **14-16 microns** in diameter, with staple lengths of **34-40 mm**, making it softer, warmer, and more durable than cashmere from other regions.

According to industry data, over 70% of the world's cashmere originates from Inner Mongolia, with Ordos accounting for a significant share. However, not all cashmere labeled as "Ordos" is genuine. Some suppliers blend cheaper fibers (wool, nylon, or lower-grade cashmere from other provinces) and still claim Ordos origin.

## 5 Steps to Verify Ordos Cashmere Origin

### Step 1: Check Certifications and Traceability

A reliable **Ordos cashmere manufacturer** should provide traceability documentation. Look for:

- **ISO 9001 (Quality Management)**: Ensures consistent production standards.
- **OEKO-TEX Standard 100**: Certifies no harmful chemicals in the final product.
- **GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard)**: If organic cashmere is claimed.
- **SCS Global Services certification**: Verifies fiber content and origin.

Ask the supplier for a **certificate of origin** from a recognized body. For example, the China National Textile and Apparel Council (CNTAC) issues origin certificates for Inner Mongolian cashmere. Also request **batch traceability reports** that track fiber from goat to garment.

**Pro tip**: Genuine Ordos cashmere often has a unique batch number that can be verified with the supplier's internal system or a third-party database.

### Step 2: Perform Lab Testing

Visual inspection isn't enough. Send samples to an accredited lab for fiber analysis. Key tests include:

- **Fiber diameter test**: Genuine Ordos cashmere averages 14-16 microns. Anything above 18 microns is likely lower-grade or blended.
- **Fiber content analysis**: Detects wool, nylon, or other adulterants. ISO 1833 or AATCC 20A methods are standard.
- **Staple length test**: Ordos cashmere has staple lengths of 34-40 mm. Shorter fibers (<30 mm) indicate lower quality.
- **Microscopic examination**: Cashmere fibers have a distinct scale structure that differs from wool or synthetics.

Reputable labs include **SGS**, **Intertek**, and **Bureau Veritas**. Cost per test is typically $50-150, a small price to avoid a costly mislabeling scandal.

### Step 3: Audit the Supplier's Manufacturing Base

A legitimate **Inner Mongolia cashmere factory** should have a physical presence in Ordos or nearby. Request a **factory audit** or video tour. Key things to verify:

- **Location**: Is the factory in Ordos city or surrounding counties (e.g., Dongsheng, Dalad Banner)?
- **Production capacity**: Ask about monthly output of raw cashmere, yarn, or garments. For example, DONGXIAO Cashmere processes **120 tons of raw cashmere annually**, with **MOQs starting at 200 kg for yarn and 500 pieces for garments**.
- **Equipment**: Look for modern dehairing, spinning, and knitting machines. Old machinery often produces inconsistent quality.
- **Worker expertise**: Skilled artisans are essential for high-end cashmere. Check if the factory employs experienced craftsmen.

If the supplier hesitates to share location details or provide a virtual tour, that's a red flag.

### Step 5: Ask the Right Questions

When vetting a **B2B cashmere sourcing** partner, ask:

- "Can you provide a certificate of origin for this batch?"
- "What is the micron count and staple length?"
- "Do you have third-party lab test reports?"
- "Can we visit your factory in Ordos?"
- "What is your traceability system from goat to garment?"
- "How do you ensure no wool or synthetic fibers are mixed in?"

A transparent supplier will answer these directly. Evasive answers or promises of "we'll send later" are warning signs.

## Common Mislabeling Scams to Avoid

- **"Inner Mongolia" but not Ordos**: Some suppliers source cashmere from other parts of Inner Mongolia (e.g., Hulunbuir, Xilingol). While still Chinese cashmere, it's often coarser (16-19 microns) and less consistent. Insist on Ordos-specific origin.
- **Blended cashmere sold as pure**: A garment labeled "100% cashmere" may contain up to 5% wool or nylon legally in some countries, but premium buyers expect zero blending. Lab test to confirm.
- **Fake certificates**: Some suppliers create counterfeit certificates. Verify with the issuing body—e.g., check the OEKO-TEX certificate number on their official website.
- **Recycled cashmere**: Recycled fibers are shorter and weaker. If a supplier claims "new cashmere" but prices are low, ask for a virgin fiber certificate.
