# Dehaired Cashmere Tops: The Complete B2B Buyer's Guide

**Dehaired cashmere** is the post-processed fiber that spinners actually work with. It comes from combing out guard hair and impurities from raw (greasy) cashmere. The quality of this step determines whether your finished yarn is luxury-grade or full of pills after three wears.

This guide covers what separates premium dehaired cashmere from commodity fiber.

## What "dehaired" actually means

Raw cashmere combed from goats is called **greasy cashmere** — a tangled mass of fine underdown (the valuable part), thick guard hair (useless for spinning), lanolin, dirt, and vegetable matter.

**Dehairing** is the mechanical process of separating the fine underdown from the guard hair. The result is a clean, aligned **cashmere top** (also called a combed top or roving) that can be drawn into yarn.

The process is purely mechanical — modern dehaiiring lines use no chemicals, no water, no dye. Italian-made Capstan and similar equipment have been the industry standard for 30+ years.

## The 4 grades of dehaired cashmere

After dehairing, fiber is sorted by **micron count** (fiber diameter, measured in micrometers):

| Grade | Micron | Use case | Price (USD/kg) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Superfine white** | 14.5-15.5μm | Luxury knitwear, fine-gauge scarves | $120-160 |
| **Standard white** | 15.0-16.0μm | Mid-luxury knitwear, premium blending | $90-115 |
| **Brown (qing)** | 15.5-16.5μm | Heathered yarns, workwear | $80-100 |
| **Purple (zi)** | 16.0-17.5μm | Felted products, low-end blends | $65-85 |

For B2B buyers sourcing for luxury or mid-luxury brands, **Superfine white** and **Standard white** are 90% of the market.

## 5 quality indicators in dehaired cashmere

A premium dehaired cashmere top has the following characteristics. Test any sample for these before committing to a bulk order.

### 1. **Fiber parallel alignment**

A properly combed top has fibers running roughly parallel along its length. Hold a small tuft up to the light — you should see a uniform sheen without visible "tangles" or "clumps."

A poorly dehaired top has fibers in random orientations, making it harder to spin and resulting in weaker, hairier yarn.

### 2. **Residual guard hair content**

Under 40x magnification (a $30 USB microscope works), count guard hair fibers in a 1g sample. Premium dehaired cashmere should have **< 1% guard hair by count**. Anything above 3% suggests incomplete dehairing.

Guard hair in finished yarn shows up as "frizzies" — thick, straight fibers that don't take dye the same as cashmere and create a coarse, itchy hand feel.

### 3. **Micron uniformity (CV%)**

Beyond the mean micron count, look at the **coefficient of variation** (CV%) — the standard deviation divided by the mean. A premium dehaired top has **CV < 20%**. Anything above 25% means the fiber is mixed (different goats, different ages, or blended batches).

A high CV shows up in your yarn as inconsistent dyeing and uneven texture — some fibers absorb dye differently, creating a "heathered" look you didn't intend.

### 4. **Moisture content**

Premium dehaired cashmere should have **< 12% moisture content** (regain). Above 15% means:
- The fiber wasn't properly dried
- You're paying for water weight, not fiber
- Risk of mold in storage

A simple moisture meter ($100-200) gives instant readings. Any supplier refusing to provide moisture data is hiding something.

### 5. **Color uniformity within bale**

Real "white" cashmere has subtle color variation — different goats, different body parts, different ages contribute. Perfectly uniform bright-white fiber has been **bleached**, which damages the cuticle and shortens fiber life.

Take 5-10 random samples from different parts of a bale. Subtle variation (ivory to light gray) is normal. Identical bright white across all samples suggests processing.

## Combed top vs. open top: which to choose?

Dehaired cashmere comes in two forms:

- **Combed top** (roving): Fibers are parallel-aligned into a continuous rope. Used for **worsted spinning** (smooth, strong yarn for machine knitting and weaving).
- **Open top** (sliver): Fibers are randomized. Used for **woolen spinning** (soft, lofty yarn for hand knitting and chunky sweaters).

For machine knitting 12+ gauge, you want combed top. For hand knitting or 5-7 gauge chunky, open top is fine.
