# How B2B Cashmere Buyers Verify Ordos Origin and Avoid Mislabeling: A Sourcing Guide

## Introduction

The global cashmere market is valued at over $4 billion, with Inner Mongolia producing roughly 70% of the world's raw cashmere. Ordos, a city in the heart of the region, has become synonymous with premium cashmere due to its unique climate and long history of goat breeding. However, mislabeling—where inferior fibers are sold as "Ordos cashmere"—remains a persistent challenge for B2B buyers.

This guide provides a practical framework for verifying Ordos origin, avoiding counterfeit products, and building a reliable supply chain. Whether you're a brand buyer, trading company, or manufacturer, these steps will help protect your reputation and investment.

## Why Ordos Cashmere Matters

The Ordos region (Ejin Horo Banner, Dalad Banner, and surrounding areas) produces cashmere with distinct characteristics:

- **Fiber fineness**: 14.5–16.5 microns (vs. 15.5–17.5 microns for other regions)
- **Staple length**: 32–38 mm for raw cashmere
- **Low coarse hair content**: <1% (industry standard is 2%)
- **Natural color spectrum**: White, light gray, and brown varieties

These traits result in softer, lighter, and more durable yarns—making Ordos cashmere a premium choice for luxury brands.

## Step 1: Verify Supplier Credentials

Before any transaction, confirm your supplier's legitimacy:

- **Business license**: Check registration with Ordos Municipal Administration for Market Regulation
- **Factory audit**: Request on-site visit or third-party audit (e.g., SGS, Bureau Veritas)
- **Export history**: Ask for customs documentation showing past shipments
- **Certifications**: Look for ISO 9001:2015 (quality management), OEKO-TEX Standard 100 (chemical safety), and GOTS (organic if applicable)

## Step 2: Request Fiber Testing

Mislabeling often involves mixing cashmere with lower-grade fibers like lambswool, yak, or synthetic materials. Insist on independent lab testing:

- **Microscopic analysis**: Identifies fiber type and diameter distribution
- **Chemical composition test**: Detects synthetic blends (e.g., nylon, polyester)
- **Fiber diameter test**: Measures micron count (ASTM D2130 standard)
- **Staple length test**: Ensures consistency (ISO 6989 standard)

**Recommended labs**: SGS, Intertek, or Bureau Veritas. Request certificates for each lot.

## Step 3: Trace the Supply Chain

Ordos origin isn't just about where the factory is located—it's about the raw material source. Implement traceability:

- **Raw material procurement**: Ask for proof of purchase from Ordos herders or local collection stations
- **Processing records**: Dehairing, dyeing, and spinning should occur in Ordos or nearby Inner Mongolia facilities
- **Batch tracking**: Each lot should have a unique ID linking to origin documentation

**Pro tip**: Visit the Ordos Cashmere Exchange (the world's largest cashmere trading platform) to verify raw material pricing and availability.

## Step 4: Evaluate Physical Quality

Even without lab equipment, you can assess basic quality:

- **Hand feel**: Authentic Ordos cashmere is exceptionally soft, with a slight "dry" finish
- **Burn test**: Cashmere smells like burnt hair (not plastic) and forms a brittle ash
- **Tensile strength**: High-quality cashmere yarns have 10–15% elongation before breaking
- **Color consistency**: Dye should be uniform, with no bleeding

## Step 5: Understand Pricing Red Flags

Ordos cashmere commands a premium. Be wary of prices that are too low:

- **Raw cashmere**: $60–$90 per kg (2024 market range)
- **Spun yarn**: $80–$150 per kg (depending on micron count and twist)
- **Finished garments**: $40–$120 per piece (depending on design and weight)

If a supplier offers 100% cashmere yarn at $50/kg, it's likely blended or from a lower-quality source.

## Step 6: Check Certifications and Compliance

Reputable Ordos manufacturers maintain certifications that prove origin and quality:

- **ISO 9001:2015**: Quality management system
- **OEKO-TEX Standard 100**: No harmful chemicals
- **GOTS**: Organic cashmere (if applicable)
- **SFA (Sustainable Fibre Alliance)**: Ethical sourcing and animal welfare
- **CCMI (Cashmere and Camel Hair Manufacturers Institute)**: Membership indicates commitment to authenticity

## Step 7: Conduct a Factory Visit

Nothing beats seeing the operation firsthand. During a visit:

- **Raw material storage**: Check for separate bins for different origins
- **Production line**: Observe dehairing, carding, and spinning processes
- **Quality control**: Ask about in-house testing frequency (e.g., every batch vs. random sampling)
- **Worker conditions**: Ensure compliance with labor laws (ILO standards)

**Travel tip**: Ordos is accessible via Ordos Ejin Horo Airport (DSN) with direct flights from Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou.

## Step 8: Use Blockchain or Digital Tracking

Some advanced manufacturers now offer blockchain-based traceability. This isn't yet standard but is growing:

- **QR codes** on yarn cones or garment labels linking to origin data
- **Digital certificates** from platforms like TextileGenesis or TrustTrace
- **Smart contracts** for automated compliance verification

Ask your supplier if they offer such solutions—it's a sign of transparency.

## Common Mislabeling Schemes to Watch For

1. **"Mongolian cashmere"** from non-Ordos sources (e.g., Chifeng, Xilingol)
2. **"Inner Mongolian cashmere"** mixed with Chinese cashmere from other provinces
3. **"Cashmere blend"** with >20% non-cashmere fibers
4. **Recycled cashmere** sold as virgin
5. **Counterfeit certificates** from unknown labs

## Legal Recourse and Documentation

If you discover mislabeling:

- **Contract terms**: Include penalty clauses for non-compliance (e.g., 3x value of defective goods)
- **Inspection rights**: Reserve the right to inspect at any stage
- **Dispute resolution**: Specify arbitration in a neutral jurisdiction (e.g., Singapore or Hong Kong)
- **Insurance**: Consider trade credit insurance for high-value orders
